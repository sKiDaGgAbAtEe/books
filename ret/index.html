Abstract

Classical game theory models decision-making through rational choice and payoff maximization, culminating in the Nash Equilibrium: a stable state from which no player can unilaterally deviate. However, these models fail to explain a wide range of empirically observed human behaviors, including cooperation under risk, symbolic focal point selection, anticipatory strategy shifts, and memetic collapse.

We introduce Resonant Equilibrium Theory (RET) as a field-theoretic expansion of Nash dynamics, grounded in the framework of ΔHV (Delta Harmonic Vector) analysis. RET posits that equilibria emerge not only from payoff gradients but also from multidimensional field variables including symbolic compression, temporal rhythm, phase alignment, and field curvature.

This expanded model reveals five distinct equilibrium mechanisms: (1) Gradient-Based Basins, (2) Resonance-Locked Basins, (3) Recursively-Evoked Basins, (4) Interference Zones, and (5) Precessive Shifts. Nash equilibria are shown to be the limiting case of RET where coherence costs vanish. RET thus retains classical validity while offering a structurally sound framework for modeling agent behavior in complex, meaning-rich environments.

Keywords: game theory, Nash equilibrium, field theory, coherence, symbolic alignment, temporal entrainment, multi-agent systems, ΔHV analysis

1. Introduction: What is RET?

Resonant Equilibrium Theory (RET) extends classical game theory by incorporating symbolic, temporal, and phase-based coherence into strategic decision-making. While Nash equilibrium predicts outcomes based solely on payoffs, RET explains how meaning, timing, and synchronization fundamentally shape which equilibria emerge.

1.1 The Core Innovation

RET modifies classical utility with coherence costs:

ũi = ui - Ci

where Ci penalizes misalignment across four dimensions:

  1. Symbolic (S) — shared meanings and narratives
  2. Temporal (R) — rhythmic entrainment
  3. Phase (δφ) — temporal synchronization
  4. Field (H) — attractor basin structure
When coherence costs vanish (Ci = 0), RET reduces exactly to Nash equilibrium.
Thus: Nash ⊂ RET

Nash is the special case; RET is the general framework.

1.2 Why Nash Fails

Nash Equilibrium cannot explain:

  • Communication effects: Why 10 minutes of discussion increases cooperation from 40% to 72% in Prisoner's Dilemma (Dawes et al., 1977)
  • Framing effects: Why identical games yield 33% vs 66% cooperation based solely on labels like "Wall Street" vs "Community" (Liberman et al., 2004)
  • Focal points: Why agents converge on specific equilibria when multiple exist (Schelling, 1960)
  • Temporal coordination: Why rhythmic cues facilitate cooperation beyond payoff predictions (Wiltermuth & Heath, 2009)

Nash theory says these factors should be irrelevant. Empirical data shows they're decisive.

RET Signature Principle: "The equilibrium that emerges is the one most resonant with the field—not the one with the highest reward."

def ret_decision(payoffs: dict, dhv_state: DHVState, temperature: float = 0.6) -> dict: """ Make RET-based decision with coherence-adjusted utility Args: payoffs: dict mapping actions to utilities dhv_state: Current ΔHV state temperature: Softmax temperature for stochastic decisions Returns: dict with probabilities for each action """ u_max = max(payoffs.values()) coherence_cost = dhv_state.coherence_cost(u_max) # Adjust utilities adjusted = { action: u - coherence_cost for action, u in payoffs.items() } # Softmax over adjusted utilities exp_utils = { action: np.exp(u / temperature) for action, u in adjusted.items() } Z = sum(exp_utils.values()) return { action: exp_u / Z for action, exp_u in exp_utils.items() }
