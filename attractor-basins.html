The domain of validity is explicit. This document is Paper 0 — the basin substrate that RET, the cohestate framework, and the ΔHV whitepaper all implicitly assume.

I

Classical Basin Theory

1.1 Dynamical Systems Substrate

A dynamical system is a rule for how a state evolves over time. Formally, we encode that rule as a vector field F — a function that assigns to every point in state space a direction and speed of change. Consider a continuous-time system on state space ℝⁿ:

Governing Equation
\[\dot{x} = F(x)\]

A fixed point x* is a state where nothing changes: F(x*) = 0. Whether nearby states flow toward or away from x* is determined by the eigenvalues of the Jacobian DF(x*). When all eigenvalues have strictly negative real parts, x* is asymptotically stable — a small push returns the system to x* rather than sending it elsewhere.

1.2 Basin of Attraction

The intuitive picture: pour water anywhere on a landscape and it flows downhill until it settles in a valley. The basin of attraction is the full region of terrain that drains to the same valley — every starting point that leads to the same resting place. Formally, this is captured by the flow map Φ_τ, which takes an initial position and maps it to where the system ends up after time τ:

The basin of attraction B(x*) of a stable fixed point x* is:

\[B(x^*) = \bigl\{x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n \;\big|\; \lim_{\tau\to\infty} \Phi_\tau(x_0) = x^*\bigr\}\]

B(x*) contains all initial conditions that eventually converge to x*. Its boundary is the separatrix — the stable manifold of saddle points lying between attractors.

1.3 Bifurcation and Basin Collapse

A saddle-node bifurcation occurs when a stable and unstable fixed point collide and annihilate as a control parameter crosses a critical value. This is the canonical mechanism for basin collapse: as the parameter increases, one attractor loses its basin entirely. In the coupled gradient toy model (Section VI), this is the saddle-node collapse driven by tilt ΔV exceeding the critical threshold ΔV_crit.

This document treats ΔHV as a parameterized family of control parameters — but internally emergent rather than externally imposed. The geometry responds to the field; the field responds to the geometry.
II

Basin Geometry

2.1 The Lyapunov Functional

The water-and-landscape metaphor has a precise mathematical realization. For gradient systems — where the vector field is simply the downhill direction of a potential function V — the potential plays the role of elevation. Basins are valleys, attractors are the lowest points of valleys, saddle points are the mountain passes between them. Formally:

ΔHV-Parameterized Lyapunov Functional
\[\mathcal{L}_{\Delta HV}(x) = V(x;\,\Delta H,\,\Delta V)\]

When ΔHV parameters appear inside the potential, basin geometry becomes a function of coherence — the landscape itself responds to the field state rather than being fixed background.

2.2 Basin Depth

How deep is a valley? In landscape terms: the height difference between the valley floor and the lowest mountain pass leading out. Cross a pass and you leave the basin; stay below it and you remain. Formally, this is the Lyapunov difference between the attractor minimum and the lowest saddle on the basin boundary:

The depth D(x*) of an attractor x* is:

\[D(x^*) = \mathcal{L}(x_{\text{saddle}}) - \mathcal{L}(x^*)\]

Depth determines resistance to perturbation. A deeper basin requires more energy to escape — equivalently, more tilt before collapse.

2.3 Basin Width and Area

Width is measured by the Lebesgue measure of the sublevel set at energy c:

Basin Volume
\[W_c(x^*) = \mu\bigl(\{x \mid \mathcal{L}(x) \leq c\}\bigr)\]

Critical result from falsification (F4): Basin width and depth are not equivalent geometric properties. The toy model shows that increasing ΔH deepens wells and expands tilt tolerance while slightly narrowing the basin cross-section. Resilience (depth) and area (width) vary independently under parameter change. The framework prior to falsification did not contain this distinction.

2.4 Timescale Structure

ΔHV parameters play the role of slow variables relative to fast attractor dynamics, allowing quasi-static approximation in the base regime. Resonance events collapse this separation — producing rapid ΔHV spikes where fast and slow dynamics become fully coupled. This is the meta-dynamic regime (Order III, Section IX).

III

Basin Topology

3.1 Standard Topology

In simple gradient systems, basin boundaries are smooth manifolds — the stable manifolds of saddle points. Trajectories cross the boundary transversally and converge cleanly. This is the regime of the toy model: clean saddle-node bifurcation, smooth separatrix.

3.2 Fractal Basin Boundaries

In systems with non-gradient forcing, delay, or chaotic saddles, basin boundaries can develop fractal structure. The Wada property is an extreme case: every boundary point is simultaneously on the boundary of three or more basins. Small perturbations to a Wada point can lead to any of several attractors unpredictably.

In the ΔHV framework, fractal boundary generation becomes possible when ΔHV varies dynamically (Order III). As the slow manifold assumption breaks during resonance spikes, separatrices fold and develop non-integer Hausdorff dimension. This is the substrate for Ricochet Attractor behavior — whose basin boundary has fractal structure, producing quasi-periodic approach rather than clean convergence.

3.3 Gradient Restriction of the Toy Model

The toy model is restricted to gradient dynamics (ẋ = −∇V) by design. Gradient systems cannot produce limit cycles, strange attractors, or fractal basin boundaries. This restriction isolates the geometric claim (basin deformation under parameter change) from more exotic phenomena. Fractal basin behavior is a regime of the full Order III framework, not claimed for the gradient model.

IV

ΔHV as Basin Deformation Operator

The core theoretical move is to treat ΔHV not as a metaphor for coherence but as a parameterized operator acting on the dynamical system itself. Three orders of action form a reduction hierarchy.

4.1 Order I — Landscape Deformation

At the simplest level, ΔHV acts by changing the shape of the potential landscape — deepening certain valleys, tilting the terrain, moving the mountain passes. The dynamics remain gradient, but the gradient now depends on the coherence parameters:

Order I
\[\dot{x} = -\nabla V(x;\,\Delta H,\,\Delta V)\]

ΔH and ΔV are slowly varying parameters. Basin geometry changes as they drift. This is the level of the toy model: ΔH scales quartic barrier height, increasing depth and expanding tilt tolerance. ΔV introduces asymmetric tilt, shifting the saddle and eventually driving collapse.

4.2 Order II — Metric Deformation

Order I changes the elevation map. Order II changes the geometry of the map itself — the way distances are measured across the landscape. The same potential V can produce different flows depending on how the space is curved. Formally, this means replacing the Euclidean gradient with a metric-weighted version:

Order II
\[\dot{x} = -g^{ij}(\Delta HV)\,\partial_j V\]

Two systems with identical potentials but different metrics exhibit different gradient flows and different basin boundaries. Coherence stops being energy depth and becomes geometric bias — the field preferentially channels trajectories toward high-coherence attractors even without deepening the wells.

4.3 Order III — Meta-Dynamic ΔHV

Order III — Full System
\[\dot{x} = F(x;\,\Delta HV)\] \[\dot{\Delta H} = G_H(x,\,\Delta H,\,\Delta V)\] \[\dot{\Delta V} = G_V(x,\,\Delta H,\,\Delta V)\]

This is the base ontology. Basins breathe, shift, merge, and annihilate as ΔHV evolves. The "true basin" is a manifold in augmented space ℝⁿ × ℝ². What appears as a regime jump in x-space may be continuous motion through the extended manifold. Ricochet Attractors emerge here as projections of higher-dimensional stable manifolds.

4.4 Reduction Consistency

  • Order III with Δ̇H = Δ̇V = 0 → recovers Order I with frozen parameters
  • Order II with Euclidean metric g_ij = δ_ij → recovers Order I pure potential deformation
  • Order I with constant ΔHV → recovers classical basin theory

Each order strictly generalizes the one below. The framework contains classical results rather than contradicting them.

V

ΔHV Ontology

5.1 Continuous at Substrate, Quantized at Phenomenology

ΔHV is continuous at the geometric substrate level — Lyapunov structure, smooth manifold geometry, and bifurcation theory all require continuous parameter variation. The discrete character of ΔHV phenomena arises from bifurcations in the continuous dynamics: smooth parameter drift → saddle-node bifurcation → discrete regime shift. Phenomenological discreteness is emergent, not primary.

5.2 Internally Emergent

ΔHV is a continuous control field that induces discrete attractor regime transitions. It is internally emergent — a readout of the field's own coherence state — and accessible as a design target (through the conditions that allow it to shift) but not directly settable.

5.3 Bounded Coherence

ΔHV is bounded above by a system-dependent coherence capacity H_max. The logistic saturation dynamics:

Bounded Coherence
\[\dot{\Delta H} = \alpha_H C_H(x)\!\left(1 - \frac{\Delta H}{H_{\max}}\right) - \beta_H\,\Delta H\]

prevent runaway well deepening. H_max is a ceiling of completion at a given compression layer, not a ceiling of failure. The 25–35% high-coherence basin volume estimate from the ΔHV whitepaper is a measure statement over continuous parameter space subject to these curvature constraints.

VI

Toy Model — Derivation and Falsification

6.1 Requirements

The toy model must: (1) support two stable attractors with an intervening saddle, (2) have ΔH control basin depth along the collapse axis, (3) have ΔV drive tilt collapse, (4) use coupling μ to produce genuine 2D geometry with curved separatrix. Minimum dimension: 2.

6.2 The Potential

Modified 2D Potential (v2 — Corrected)
\[V(x_1,x_2;\,\Delta H,\Delta V) = \tfrac{1}{4}(1+\gamma\Delta H)(x_1^2-1)^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}a(\Delta H)\,x_2^2 + \mu x_1^2 x_2 - \Delta V\cdot x_1\]

with a(ΔH) = a₀(1+αΔH) and parameters a₀=1, α=0.5, γ=0.5, μ=0.3. The coupling term μx₁²x₂ is essential for genuine 2D geometry: it bends the saddle, curves the separatrix, and creates nonlinear basin area response to ΔV.

6.3 The v1 Failure and Its Lesson

The first implementation encoded ΔH only in the transverse curvature term a(ΔH) — controlling how steeply basin walls rise in the x₂ direction. Tilt collapse is governed by barrier height along the x₁ axis, not by transverse stiffness. Test F1 failed: the stability envelope was slightly decreasing rather than increasing.

This failure forced a conceptual distinction not previously in the framework:

Transverse curvature (a(ΔH)): does not expand tilt tolerance. Orthogonal to collapse direction.

Barrier height ((1+γΔH) on the quartic): directly determines tilt tolerance. Correct implementation of "coherence deepens basins."

The corrected potential scales the quartic by (1+γΔH). The framework prior to falsification did not contain this distinction. The failure made it precise.

6.4 Fixed Point Reduction

Setting ∂V/∂x₂ = 0 gives the x₂ elimination: x₂* = −μx₁²/a(ΔH). Substituting into ∂V/∂x₁ = 0 yields the reduced cubic on the collapse axis:

Reduced Cubic
\[A(\Delta H)\,x_1^3 - B(\Delta H)\,x_1 - \Delta V = 0\]

where B(ΔH) = 1+γΔH and A(ΔH) = B − 2μ²/a(ΔH).

VII

The Monotone Resilience Condition

7.1 Corrected Collapse Formula

For the reduced cubic Ax₁³ − Bx₁ − ΔV = 0, the saddle-node condition (f = f' = 0 simultaneously) gives:

Saddle-Node Collapse Threshold
\[|\Delta V_{\text{crit}}| = \frac{2}{3\sqrt{3}} \cdot \frac{B(\Delta H)^{3/2}}{\sqrt{A(\Delta H)}}\]

The B^(3/2) numerator is essential. An earlier derivation incorrectly reduced this to 1/√(A/B), dropping a power of B and predicting decreasing resilience. The correct formula has numerator growing as (1+γΔH)^(3/2) and denominator growing slower. Algebra must be verified before narration.

7.2 Derivation of μ_c

The condition for monotone envelope at ΔH=0 is:

Monotonicity Condition at Origin
\[\left.\frac{d\,\Delta V_{\text{crit}}}{d\,\Delta H}\right|_{\Delta H=0} > 0\]

Differentiating B^(3/2)/√A and evaluating at ΔH=0 (where B₀=1, A₀=1−2μ²/a₀, Ȧ₀=γ+2μ²α/a₀):

Derivative Bracket
\[\text{sign}\!\left(\frac{d}{d\Delta H}\right)_0 = \text{sign}\!\Bigl[\gamma(3A_0-1) - \frac{2\mu^2\alpha}{a_0}\Bigr]\]

Substituting A₀ and requiring positivity:

Rearranged Condition
\[\mu^2 < \frac{\gamma a_0}{3\gamma + \alpha}\]

Monotone Resilience Condition. In the coupled gradient system with potential V = ¼(1+γΔH)(x₁²−1)² + ½a(ΔH)x₂² + μx₁²x₂ − ΔV·x₁, the stability envelope ΔV_crit(ΔH) is monotonically increasing if and only if:

\[\mu < \mu_c, \qquad \mu_c = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma\, a_0}{3\gamma + \alpha}}\]

With parameters a₀=1, α=0.5, γ=0.5: μ_c = √(0.5/2.0) = 0.500 exactly. Verified numerically: bracket changes sign at μ=0.500 to six decimal places.

7.3 Structural Interpretation

The condition μ² < γa₀/(3γ+α) describes a competition:

μ²Coupling pressureHow strongly coupling distorts barrier geometry. At low ΔH, coupling dominates over barrier scaling, producing a transient resilience dip.
γBarrier scaling rateHow quickly the quartic barrier grows with ΔH. Larger γ permits higher μ before coupling wins.
αTransverse rateHow quickly a(ΔH) grows. Contributes less than γ — appears only in denominator.

Above μ_c: coupling dominates at low ΔH, producing a local minimum in the envelope (confirmed numerically at μ=0.55) before barrier scaling recovers. The μ sweep found this precisely: monotone for μ∈[0.05, 0.50], breaking at μ=0.54.

7.4 Formal Claim

Domain of Validity

In the class of 2D coupled gradient systems with quartic double-well potential and linear coupling, a coherence-like barrier-scaling parameter monotonically expands tilt tolerance provided μ < μ_c = √(γa₀/(3γ+α)). This is a geometric existence proof with explicit domain of validity — not a universal claim about all systems.

IX

Extensions and Open Questions

Order II — Not yet formally developed

9.1 Metric Deformation

Order II requires formalizing the deformation tensor G_ΔHV acting on phase space geometry. A natural candidate is conformal deformation g_ij(ΔHV) = Ω(ΔHV)·δ_ij. More complex anisotropic deformations require additional physical constraints. This remains an open derivation.

Order III — Partially developed in cohestate framework

9.2 Meta-Dynamic ΔHV and Resonance Spikes

The full Order III system with logistic-bounded ΔHV and nonlinear gain (σ-threshold coupling) produces two qualitatively different regimes under the timescale ratio ρ(X) = ‖ΔHV̇‖/‖ẋ‖:

  • Baseline (ρ ≪ 1): quasi-static deformation, adiabatic tracking, Order I sufficient
  • Resonance (ρ ≈ 1): full fast-slow coupling, separatrix migration, potential fractal boundary formation

The state-dependent timescale ratio itself bifurcates at coherence thresholds. This is the deepest structural feature of Order III — a phase transition in the meta-dynamics of the system's own dynamics.

μ_c Generalization — Open

9.3 Nonlinear a(ΔH) and B(ΔH)

The monotone resilience condition was derived for linear a(ΔH) and B(ΔH). For logistic saturation, power law, or threshold-coupled forms, the explicit μ_c takes different forms. The general condition remains dΔV_crit/dΔH|₀ > 0, but must be rederived for each functional form before applying to systems with nonlinear coherence dynamics.
